⏺ Yes — that's expected and worth killing. At 8 bits the codebook has 256
  entries and the cost scales with n_levels:

  Why it's slow:
  - _lloyd_max_1d (line 60): samples[:, None] - centroids[None, :] materializes
  an (n_samples, 256) array every iteration, 25 iters.
  - Total samples across 9 levels ≈ 20k × 511 ≈ 10M points for fitting.
  - Then _quantize_to_codebook (line 76) does the same (N, d/2^(k+1), 256)
  broadcast for all 186k vectors during encode(). Level 0 alone is a (186k, 256,
   256) array ≈ 48 GB in float32 — you're almost certainly swapping to disk.
  - 2 bits → 4-entry codebook (fast). 8 bits → 256-entry codebook (64× more work
   + memory). It's ~cubic in bits in practice.

  Recommendation — kill it. Your interesting range is 1–4 bits (that's where the
   cross-modal vs same-modal story lives). PolarQuant at 8 bits is essentially
  lossless, so it's not a load-bearing datapoint. Options:

  1. Cap PolarQuant/TurboQuant sweep at 4 bits (cleanest). 8-bit FAISS-PQ and
  8-bit QJL are cheap and still give you the "high-bit" ceiling in plots.
  2. Fix the memory blowup first if you really want 8-bit — swap the
  _lloyd_max_1d argmin + _quantize_to_codebook for scipy.cluster.vq.vq or chunk
  the broadcast over N. Probably 10–30× faster and no swap.

  For the sweep, I'd drop 8-bit from PolarQuant/TurboQuant and carry on.

  ---
  Findings

  1. Cross-modal fragility — confirmed. At 1 bit/dim, T4 (image→image) loses
  almost nothing (QJL R@10 = 0.889 vs 1.000 uncompressed, –11%). T1 (text→image)
   collapses (0.154 vs 0.498, –69%). The gap persists across every method at
  every low bit-width — this is the paper-worthy finding.

  2. PolarQuant dominates in the 3–4 bit regime. PolarQuant@4 bits recovers ~97%
   of uncompressed R@10 on T1 (0.483 vs 0.498), while using 8× less memory.
  QJL@4 recovers only ~70%.

  3. FAISS-PQ is the strongest low-bit baseline. At 1 bit/dim, FAISS-PQ T1 =
  0.337, more than 2× QJL (0.154) and PolarQuant (0.131). The Lloyd-optimal
  learned codebook beats random projection and analytic polar quantization at
  aggressive compression ratios.

  4. TurboQuant underperforms its components — hypothesis rejected for CLIP. At
  2 bits TurboQuant gives T1=0.157 vs PolarQuant 0.349 and QJL 0.248. The
  polar-angles-at-bits-1 + 1-bit QJL residual construction appears to give up
  too much in angle resolution (at 2 bits TurboQuant uses only a 2-entry angle
  codebook) for the QJL residual to recover. This deviates from the paper's
  KV-cache results — consistent with our earlier note that our implementation is
   not paper-faithful (empirical Lloyd-Max vs theoretical Beta-distribution
  quantizer, single codebook per level, reconstruction-based estimator).

  5. T3 (text→text) is compression-insensitive. All methods land at 0.44–0.53
  R@10 regardless of bits, because the uncompressed ceiling is already 0.528 —
  there's no headroom for compression to hurt. This reflects inherent ambiguity
  in caption retrieval (5 captions/image share content), not compression
  quality. Be careful not to over-read the "same-modal robustness" story into
  T3.

  ---
  Threats to validity (put these in the limitations section)

  - Our PolarQuant/TurboQuant implementations deviate from the papers (Lloyd-Max
   fit on empirical distribution rather than closed-form Beta; single rotation;
  reconstruction-based estimator). A paper-faithful TurboQuant might do better.
  - Only 1000 queries per task — adequate for R@10 stability but we haven't
  computed CIs on R@1.
  - CLIP ViT-B/32 only. Larger CLIP backbones have different embedding
  geometries.
  - 8-bit PolarQuant/TurboQuant not evaluated (OOM).
